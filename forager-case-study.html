Case Study

A field guide that fits in your pocket

Designing a mobile companion for foragers who need trustworthy safety information and a way to remember where they found it — built for dirty hands, bright sunlight, and no signal.

RoleUX/UI Designer (solo)
Timeframe59 Hours
ToolsFigma
TypeConcept app, iOS/Android
01 — The Problem

Foraging info is scattered, and a wrong guess can be dangerous.

Foragers currently cobble together field guides, Reddit threads, and notes apps to document their finds. No single tool combines species identification, location logging, and safety information in one place. A wrong ID isn't a minor inconvenience here — it can be genuinely dangerous. The interface needed to feel approachable and nature-forward without ever undermining how seriously it treats safety.

02 — Research

Starting from what foragers already say is broken.

Before designing, I reviewed discussion threads in foraging and camping communities to understand where existing tools fall short. This was informal, secondary research rather than structured interviews, but it surfaced consistent patterns:

  • People already stitch together multiple single-purpose apps — one for ID, another for finding nearby edible plants — because no single tool covers both.
  • Confidence scores on ID results were seen as useful for orientation, but users were clear they wouldn't act on a top match without cross-checking it themselves.
  • Several people wanted something "in the moment" — fast, situational reassurance while standing in front of an unfamiliar plant, not a reference to dig through later.
  • People who preferred physical field guides over apps pointed to the same reasons every time: detailed descriptions, prep and edibility notes, medicinal uses, and regional growing information.

A small visual mistake having a large consequence became the throughline for every safety decision that followed.

I also sketched early personas — a student foraging to save on groceries, a parent wanting to safely identify plants while camping, a chef sourcing ingredients — to keep the design grounded in different motivations, not one assumed "power user."

03 — Goals

What the design had to hold true to.

  • Make safety information the first thing a user sees — never buried in a paragraph.
  • Let logging a location feel as easy as identifying a plant, with no separate "mode" to switch into.
  • Build a personal log that turns one-off finds into a searchable, growing resource over time.
  • Design for someone outdoors, possibly with limited signal, wearing gloves, or in low light — not sitting at a desk.
04 — Key Decisions

Where the reasoning actually happened.

A quadrant number, not a linear danger scale

Every plant sits somewhere on a Danger–Usefulness Matrix — how dangerous it is, weighed against how useful it is (culinary, medicinal, ornamental) — producing four categories rather than a single sliding scale. Danger and usefulness aren't opposites: some of the most useful plants, like foxglove, a source of heart medication, are also among the most dangerous. Collapsing that nuance into one "how risky" number would hide exactly the information a forager needs most. Small icons alongside the number flag culinary, medicinal, or ornamental value, so the reasoning behind the badge is never more than a glance away. Color alone wasn't enough to carry this on its own — bright sun washes out screens, and color blindness affects roughly 1 in 12 men — so the number does the real work, with color as reinforcement.

A visible review step on new locations

The Add Location flow tells users up front that submissions are subject to review and verification. A crowdsourced location database is only useful if it's trustworthy, and foraging misinformation has real consequences. Rather than hide moderation behind the scenes, I surfaced it at the point of submission, so a delayed pin reads as a safeguard, not a bug.

Three separate search entry points

I considered a single unified search bar for everything. But someone opening the app usually already knows which of three things they want: find a place they remember, identify something new, or contribute a find. Splitting the entry point up front removes ambiguity from the rest of the flow, even at the cost of one extra tap at the start.

A persistent User Log, not just search history

A running list of "things I've found" is more valuable buried in history a user has to dig for. Framing it as a personal field log, with edibility badges visible inline, turns the app from a lookup tool into something with ongoing value — the more it's used, the more useful it becomes.

05 — Solution Walkthrough

The flow, screen by screen.

Home

A map-first view with a prominent search bar and a large capture button front and center — the two things a forager does most, reachable without a menu.

Search entry point

Tapping search reveals three clear paths — location, plant, or add a new location — so there's no ambiguity about what each does before committing to it.

Plant ID page

Leads with the photo and edibility badge, then common name, scientific name, and a plain-language description — safety and identity before anything decorative. A save icon lets users bookmark plants to reference again.

Add Location

A short form — coordinates, plant growth notes, an optional photo — kept deliberately minimal, since this is a step users take while standing outside, not sitting down at a desk.

Confirmation

A clear success state with two paths forward — go to the log, or return home — so users aren't left wondering whether the submission actually worked.

User Log

A running record of saved locations and identified plants, each with its edibility badge visible at a glance, and a way to remove entries that are no longer relevant.

06 — Outcome & Next Steps

Being honest about what's tested and what isn't.

This is a concept project, not a shipped product — the decisions above are based on reasoning about the use case and secondary research, not usability testing. If I took this further, the first thing I'd test is whether the review-step messaging on Add Location reduces frustration or just adds a layer of doubt at submission time. I'd also want to pressure-test the Danger-Usefulness categories themselves with people who actually forage regularly, not just design for them.
